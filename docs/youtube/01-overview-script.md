## 3 · Privacy first

`[ON SCREEN: Settings → Privacy/Security panel; the "no account / no login" screen; the QR device-sync pairing screen]`

> Let's get the most important thing out of the way first, because it's the thing most health apps get wrong. Chaos Command lives on *your* device. There is no cloud. There is no account. There's no login server quietly collecting your medical history to sell it back to an insurer someday.
>
> If you want your phone and your desktop to share data, they talk to *each other* — directly, device to device — and never through us. Sync is completely optional, and here's how it works: you scan a QR code, and the two devices pair over your *own* Wi-Fi, on your own network. Nothing leaves your home. There's no server in the middle, because there's no server at all. We can't see your data. We don't want to. It's yours, full stop.

## 4 · Data protection

`[ON SCREEN: the one-tap Logout button at the bottom of the sidebar; tap it → drops straight to the locked PIN screen (data stays safe, nothing resets); then the reproductive health tracker showing the scoped delete option in its footer]`

And because "private" doesn't just mean private from corporations — for some people it means private from the person in the next room. So Logout is big, it's one tap, and it lives at the bottom of the sidebar where your thumb already is. Tap it and you're out — instantly back to the locked screen. Your data's still there, safe behind your PIN, but no one's getting in without it. Fast exit, nothing to fumble for.

And reproductive health tracking goes one step further. It has a scoped delete that actually destroys your fertility data — not hides it, not archives it, deletes it — while leaving the rest of your tracking untouched. It asks you to confirm, because it can't be undone. Because in some places right now, that specific data can be used against you in court. We wish that weren't true. We built for the world we actually live in.

## 5 · The trackers

`[ON SCREEN: the Body / Mind / Choice menus opening; a couple of real trackers — dysautonomia episode types, seizure logging, the cardiac tracker, the anxiety tracker showing AuDHD-aware options]`

> Inside, there are more than forty-five trackers — and they're not generic one-to-ten mood sliders. They're built for the way real conditions actually behave.
>
> Track dysautonomia by episode type — orthostatic, blood pressure, GI, temperature, oxygen. Log a seizure with its aura and what the recovery felt like. Glucose, insulin, ketones, CGM timers. Cardiac, respiratory, head pain down to the hemiplegic features. Brain fog sorted by *kind* of fog. Energy in spoons, with room to flag the crash before it lands. Pain, sleep, hydration, food and allergens with separate logic for IgE allergies versus celiac versus intolerance — because those are three different things and your gut knows the difference even if your doctor doesn't. Anxiety that knows the difference between a panic attack and an autistic meltdown, because those need different interventions even though they look similar from the outside. Mental health, coping strategies, crisis planning.
>
> If your body does it differently than expected, we probably track it.

## 6 · Built-in medical safety

`[ON SCREEN: an emergency criteria card expanding — the 911 / 988 red-flag language, the "if this is happening RIGHT NOW" framing; then a second card collapsing to a small pill after being read]`

> And where a symptom can be genuinely dangerous, the app says so. Real red flags are baked right into the trackers — the patterns that mean *call 911 now*, not *mention it next month*. Status epilepticus. The chest-pain picture that could be a heart attack. The worst-headache-of-your-life that could be a bleed. Anaphylaxis pattern detection in the food tracker. 988 crisis support in the anxiety tracker.
>
> It tells you the difference between "this is happening right now, get help" and "this is over, let's document it for your specialist" — because on a bad day, that's not always a call you can make alone.
>
> Those cards collapse after you've read them — they don't lecture you every time. But if your recent entries trip an emergency marker, they come back. Because safety should be smart, not annoying.

## 7 · Routines

`[ON SCREEN: the Routines hub showing "Daily Tracking — 17 trackers" and "Eating — 3 trackers"; tap Run; show the flow — cards appearing one at a time, the "Copy last" button, the "Nothing today" button, the flow bar at the bottom chaining tracker to tracker, a checkmark appearing on completion]`

> Here's the newest piece, and honestly, it might be the one that changes whether you actually use this.
>
> On a bad day — the kind where executive function has packed its bags and left — opening seventeen separate trackers one at a time is exactly the kind of friction that makes you give up and log nothing. And then you have a gap in your data on the days that matter most. So now you can build a Routine — a named set of trackers you check together — and run the whole thing in one flow.
>
> A morning check. A meal-and-medication pass. A hydration nudge you run five times a day. Build it once, run it whenever. Each run starts fresh — it's scoped to when you tapped Run, not locked to once a day.
>
> And here's where the small things matter. If today looks like yesterday — tap "Copy last" and it clones your most recent entry. Done. Move on. If a tracker just didn't happen today — "Nothing today." That's not skipping your data. That's *logging* that nothing happened, and a good day is real data. Your doctor needs to know you had a week without seizures just as much as they need to know you had one.
>
> The flow bar at the bottom chains you tracker to tracker. You're not navigating, you're just moving forward. It's the difference between seventeen decisions and one.

## 8 · The Forge

`[ON SCREEN: Forge Builder tab — name + description; the Medical Terms search pulling up Pain/Fatigue/Nausea; the field-type row (Scale, Yes/No, Dropdown, Number, Text); adding a couple fields; Preview tab; then Save & Deploy; finally the new tracker live as a card in the Custom section]`

> Now — what if the thing your body does isn't on our list? Maybe it's rare. Maybe it's undiagnosed. Maybe it's so specifically *yours* that no app on earth was ever going to ship it. That's exactly what the Forge is for. It's a full custom-tracker builder, and you don't write a single line of code.
>
> And here's the part I'm a little proud of: it's backed by real medical dictionaries. You're not staring at a blank box wondering what to call things — you search actual medical terms. Symptoms, anatomy, conditions, measurements. Type "fatigue." Type "nausea." Type the joint that's been betraying you all week, and add it straight in.
>
> Then you choose how each field behaves: a one-to-ten scale for severity, a simple yes-or-no, a dropdown of options you write yourself, a plain number, or free text for the stuff that refuses to fit a box. And if you need something the dictionary has never even heard of — because bodies are weird and yours might be weirder — you build a fully custom field from scratch.
>
> Preview it, give it a name, and hit deploy. It lands in your Custom section instantly, ready to log. And from that moment it's a first-class citizen: it shows up in your routines, it feeds the pattern detection right alongside every built-in tracker, and you can tuck it out of sight on the days you don't need to see it.
>
> Because "there's no tracker for my thing" should never be where your tracking ends. If we didn't build it — you can.

## 9 · The personality

`[ON SCREEN: octopus confetti falling after completing a task; the Gremlin Detection pain scale dropdown ("WHY — BUTT WHY!!"); the Bristol scale ("constipation rock garden" → "Goldilocks zone"); the accessibility settings — font picker scrolling the full menu (Atkinson Hyperlegible, Lexend, OpenDyslexic, plus decorative ones), the motion/bounce slider Full → Static, the reproductive-health toggle; the "Did You Take Care of You?" self-care checklist; the celebration intensity picker (Off → Subtle → Party → CHAOS); a goblin affirmation]`

> Okay — we need to talk about the vibes, because they're load-bearing.
>
> Medical tracking is boring on a good day and miserable on a bad one. So we made it weird on purpose. Your pain scale has a Gremlin Detection mode. Your bathroom tracker uses phrases like "constipation rock garden" and "Goldilocks zone" for the Bristol scale — clinically accurate, but you're going to laugh, and laughing while logging your bowel movements means you'll actually do it tomorrow.
>
> Disability is a Venn diagram with no center — so in Chaos Command, everything that can be a setting, is. You've already seen the themes. But underneath those: a whole menu of fonts — accessibility-first ones like Atkinson Hyperlegible, Lexend, and OpenDyslexic for dyslexia, plus playful ones just for the joy of it — and every single one is bundled right into the app, so it never phones home to Google just to load a typeface. A motion slider you can turn all the way down to zero if animation triggers migraines, seizures, or vestibular symptoms — beautiful colors, no movement, it's a first-class mode, not an afterthought. Full keyboard and screen-reader support.
>
> Reproductive tracking is a single toggle. Turn it off and it's gone — not visibly hidden, not grayed out, but off your screen. And when it's on, it uses gender-neutral inclusive framing, because not everyone who tracks a cycle is a woman, and not every woman tracks a cycle, and tracking fertility should always be optional.
>
> Complete a task and octopus confetti falls from the sky — or penguin confetti, if that's more your speed. The celebration intensity goes from Off all the way up to Chaos. There's a self-care checklist called "Did You Take Care of You?" that's not guilt — it's a gentle nudge with a cheerleader familiar. And scattered through the app are goblin affirmations — tiny messages that say the thing your brain forgets on the hard days.
>
> None of this is decoration. This is compliance engineering. If the app feels good to open, you open it. If you open it, you log. If you log, you have data. If you have data, your doctor listens. The octopus confetti is a medical intervention and I will die on this hill.<break time="2s" />

## 10 · Patterns

`[ON SCREEN: the Patterns / correlations view — a correlation surfacing between food and migraines, a trigger flag, a trend chart]`

> That's the quiet superpower here. Chaos Command watches for correlations across everything you track — built-in and custom, side by side. The way your migraines follow the barometric pressure. The food that shows up two days before a flare. The sleep pattern that predicts your worst pain days.
>
> It surfaces the connections you'd never hold in your head at once. You stop feeling like you're imagining it, and start having evidence. And evidence changes the conversation.

## 11 · Reports that make professionals listen

`[ON SCREEN: the PDF report generator — picking an audience (Doctor / Attorney / Personal), then a generated clinical PDF with ICD-10 codes; show the specialty filter and tag exclusion]`

> And evidence is the whole game in a fifteen-minute appointment. From the same data, Chaos Command builds reports for three different audiences. A clinical report for your doctor — with the codes, the ninety-day trends, the specialty-relevant fields pre-selected so your endo isn't reading about your panic attacks. A legal report for an attorney — framed around functional impact and accommodation denials, the language disability claims actually need. And a plain-language version for you.
>
> It can even read the documents your providers give *you* — real medical language parsing that understands when a report says "no effusion" it means the thing is *absent*, not present. The kind of detail that makes the difference between being heard and being dismissed.
>
> And if there's something you tracked that you don't want in a particular report — a tag exclusion lets you hide it. What you ate is between you and your gut.

## 12 · The Gaslight Garage

`[ON SCREEN: the Gaslight Garage / evidence-locker screen — a logged entry with date, provider, what was said, attached document]`

Because being believed shouldn't depend on your memory holding up under pressure. And because dismissed findings have a way of turning up years later when someone finally listens.
