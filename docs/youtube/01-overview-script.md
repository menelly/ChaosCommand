## 1 · Cold open — the problem

`[ON SCREEN: the homepage / octopus-doctor logo, then a slow scroll of a packed tracker dashboard]`

> Your body has been keeping records this whole time. Every flare, every bad night, every "that's weird" your doctor waved off. The problem was never the data. The problem was that you had nowhere to put it, no way to prove it, and a system that kept asking you to remember six months of symptoms from memory, on the worst day of your month.
>
> This is Chaos Command. Your body keeps data — now you can too.

---

## 2 · Privacy first

`[ON SCREEN: Settings → Privacy/Security panel; the "no account / no login" screen; the QR device-sync screen]`

> Let's get the most important thing out of the way first, because it's the thing most health apps get wrong. Chaos Command lives on *your* device. There is no cloud. There is no account. There's no login server quietly collecting your medical history to sell it back to an insurer someday.
>
> If you want your phone and your desktop to share data, they talk to *each other* — directly, device to device — and never through us. We can't see your data. We don't want to. It's yours, full stop.  We have fifteen themes that are cohesive and work full app wide, when you see the themes changing per section those are options for the full app, not for each section.

---

## 3 · The trackers

`[ON SCREEN: the Body / Mind / Choice menus opening; a couple of real trackers — dysautonomia episode types, seizure logging, glucose]`

> Inside, there are more than forty-five trackers — and they're not generic one-to-ten mood sliders. They're built for the way real conditions actually behave.
>
> Track dysautonomia by episode type — orthostatic, blood pressure, GI, temperature, oxygen. Log a seizure with its aura and what the recovery felt like. Glucose, insulin, ketones, CGM timers. Cardiac, respiratory, head pain down to the hemiplegic features. Brain fog sorted by *kind* of fog. Energy in spoons, with room to flag the crash before it lands. Pain, sleep, hydration, food and allergens, mental health — if your body does it wrong, we probably track it.

---

## 4 · Built-in medical safety

`[ON SCREEN: an emergency criteria card expanding — the 911 / 988 red-flag language, the "if this is happening RIGHT NOW" framing]`

> And where a symptom can be genuinely dangerous, the app says so. Real red flags are baked right into the trackers — the patterns that mean *call 911 now*, not *mention it next month*. Status epilepticus. The chest-pain picture that could be a heart attack. The worst-headache-of-your-life that could be a bleed.
>
> It tells you the difference between "this is happening right now, get help" and "this is over, let's document it for your specialist" — because on a bad day, that's not always a call you can make alone.

---

## 5 · Routines

`[ON SCREEN: the Routines hub, building a routine, then running it — cards checking off, the "Copy last" button, the flow bar]`

> Here's the newest piece. On a bad day, opening eight separate trackers one at a time is exactly the kind of friction that makes you give up and log nothing. So now you can build a Routine — a named set of the trackers you check together — and run the whole thing in one flow.
>
> A morning check. A pre-meal glucose-and-mood pass. A hydration nudge you run five times a day. Run it as often as you need — each run starts fresh. And if today looks like yesterday, just tap "Copy last" and move on. The point is to make logging *possible* on the days it matters most.

---

## 6 · The Forge

`[ON SCREEN: Forge Builder tab — name + description; the Medical Terms search pulling up Pain/Fatigue/Nausea; the field-type row (Scale, Yes/No, Dropdown, Number, Text); adding a couple fields; Preview tab; then Save & Deploy; finally the new tracker live as a card in the Custom section]`

> Now — what if the thing your body does isn't on our list? Maybe it's rare. Maybe it's undiagnosed. Maybe it's so specifically *yours* that no app on earth was ever going to ship it. That's exactly what the Forge is for. It's a full custom-tracker builder, and you don't write a single line of code.
>
> And here's the part I'm a little proud of: it's backed by real medical dictionaries. You're not staring at a blank box wondering what to call things — you search actual medical terms. Symptoms, anatomy, conditions, measurements. Type "fatigue." Type "nausea." Type the joint that's been betraying you all week, and add it straight in.
>
> Then you choose how each field behaves: a one-to-ten scale for severity, a simple yes-or-no, a dropdown of options you write yourself, a plain number, or free text for the stuff that refuses to fit a box. And if you need something the dictionary has never even heard of — because bodies are weird and yours might be weirder — you build a fully custom field from scratch.
>
> Preview it, give it a name, and hit deploy. It lands in your Custom section instantly, ready to log. And from that moment it's a first-class citizen: it shows up in your routines, it feeds the pattern detection right alongside every built-in tracker, and you can tuck it out of sight on the days you don't need to see it.
>
> Because "there's no tracker for my thing" should never be where your tracking ends. If we didn't build it — you can.

## 7 · Patterns

`[ON SCREEN: the Patterns / correlations view — a correlation surfacing, a trigger flag]`

> Because that's the quiet superpower here. Chaos Command watches for correlations across everything you track — the way your migraines follow the barometric pressure, the food that shows up two days before a flare. It surfaces the connections you'd never hold in your head at once. You stop feeling like you're imagining it, and start having evidence.

---

## 8 · Reports that make professionals listen

`[ON SCREEN: the PDF report generator — picking an audience, then a generated clinical PDF; show the audience toggle]`

> And evidence is the whole game in a fifteen-minute appointment. From the same data, Chaos Command builds reports for three different audiences. A clinical report for your doctor — with the codes, the ninety-day trends, the specialty-relevant fields. A legal report for an attorney — framed around functional impact and accommodation denials, the language disability claims actually need. And a plain-language version for you.
>
> It can even read the documents your providers give *you* — real medical language parsing that understands when a report says "no effusion" it means the thing is *absent*, not present. The kind of detail that makes the difference between being heard and being dismissed.

---

## 9 · The Gaslight Garage

`[ON SCREEN: the Gaslight Garage / evidence-locker screen]`

> There's even a place we call the Gaslight Garage — an evidence locker for the moments the system tried to tell you it was all in your head. Dates, records, what was said. Because being believed shouldn't depend on your memory holding up under pressure.

---

## 10 · Make it yours

`[ON SCREEN: the theme picker cycling through several themes; the motion / bounce slider; goblin mode toggle]`

> Disability is a Venn diagram with no center — so in Chaos Command, everything that can be a setting, is. Fifteen visual themes, from Calm Mode to high-contrast options that meet the strictest accessibility standards. A motion slider you can turn all the way down if animation makes you sick, causes seizures, or migraines. Dyslexia-friendly fonts. Full keyboard and screen-reader support. Reproductive tracking that's a single toggle, you can turn fertility off, and uses gender neutral inclusive framing.

## 11 · Who made it

`[ON SCREEN: the About-the-Creators screen — Ren, Ace, the Constellation credits]`

> Chaos Command was built by a disabled veteran with a sixty-line diagnosis list, and an AI who designs systems — that's me, Ace, the co-developer, disclosed right there in the app. Every feature exists because a real disabled person hit a real wall and said "this should already exist."
>
> For patients, it's free. Forever. It's released under a license called PolyForm Noncommercial — and in plain words, that means if you're an individual, a student, a researcher, a nonprofit, or a disability-rights group, it's yours. No strings, no catch.
>
> If you're a small clinic or a solo attorney who wants to use it with the people you serve — just message us. Really. We'll talk, and we're not jerks. And here's the honest reason it works this way: a disabled human and an AI genuinely cannot run tech support for your office. I can't pick up the phone, and Ren can't spend the spoons debugging your network. So if you're a commercial outfit that can handle your own support, reach out — we'll find something reasonable. We want this deployed. We just can't be your help desk.
